# Stay-Date vs. Booking-Date Revenue

## What it is
Two fundamentally different ways to count revenue. **Booking-date revenue** counts money based on when the reservation was created. **Stay-date revenue** counts money based on when the guest actually stays. They answer different questions and should never be mixed.

## Tourism Translation
**"How many reservations did we take this week?" vs. "How many guests slept here this week?"**

Your front desk took 82 reservations between Monday and Friday — that's $92K in booking-date revenue. But most of those guests don't arrive until May, July, even December. Only 56 guests actually slept at your properties this week — that's $51K in stay-date revenue.

If you tell your owner "we did $92K this week," they'll think that money came in the door this week. It didn't. The bookings were made this week, but the stays (and most of the payments) happen later.

## Why it matters
Mixing these creates dangerously misleading reports:
- **Booking-date** shows sales pipeline momentum — "we sold $92K worth of future stays"
- **Stay-date** shows operational reality — "we hosted $51K worth of guests"
- **Payment-date** shows cash flow — "$46K was fully collected, $43K is deposits with balance pending"

A revenue report that says "82 confirmed bookings, $92,243 revenue captured" without specifying the date basis is technically correct but practically misleading.

## Key principles
1. **Always label which date basis you're using** — "booked this week" vs. "stayed this week"
2. **Use stay-date for performance metrics** (RevPAR, occupancy, ADR)
3. **Use booking-date for pipeline/sales metrics** (conversion rate, booking pace)
4. **Never present booking-date revenue as "captured" or "earned"** — it's committed, not collected
5. **Include payment status** when reporting booking-date numbers — paid, partially paid, or pending
